Global Extreme Weather Patterns

The study of extreme weather formation sits at the intersection of atmospheric dynamics, oceanography, radiation physics, and socio-environmental science. The World Climate Research Programme designates “understanding and predicting weather and climate extremes” as one of its “grand challenges,” reflecting the complexity of the underlying causal architecture.

Extreme events are not uniformly the product of a single forcing mechanism. Most arise from the convergence of multiple drivers: a background warming trend that shifts probability distributions toward more severe outcomes, superimposed upon natural modes of variability — El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO), the quasi-biennial oscillation (QBO), the polar vortex — and modulated by regional feedbacks such as sea-ice loss, soil moisture, and land use change.

Climate Change as the Primary Driver

Greenhouse Gas Forcing the Extreme Events

The central consensus narrative of contemporary climate science holds that anthropogenic greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions — primarily CO₂, CH₄, and N₂O — have fundamentally altered the thermodynamic state of the atmosphere and oceans, systematically raising baseline temperatures and amplifying moisture availability, which in turn increases the potential severity of extremes across virtually all categories.

The quantitative tools of “extreme event attribution” (EEA) have matured considerably. World Weather Attribution (WWA), the American Meteorological Society’s special collection Explaining Extreme Events from a Climate Perspective, and comparable frameworks now provide rapid post-event analyses. A synthesis of such studies found that, over the twelve-month period preceding May 2025, human-caused climate change added a worldwide average of 26 additional days of extreme heat annually.

Clausius-Clapeyron Constraint

A warmer atmosphere holds more water vapor — approximately 7% more per degree Celsius of warming according to the Clausius-Clapeyron relation. This thermodynamic amplifier intensifies precipitation extremes: the same atmospheric circulation that once produced moderate rainfall can now produce catastrophic inundation. Attribution studies examining Dubai’s April 2024 floods, among the most extreme rainfall episodes ever recorded in the Arabian Peninsula, concluded that similar synoptic situations in the current climate are associated with precipitation that is currently 10–40% more intense than it would have been in a pre-industrial climate.

Cascading Extremes

Human-induced warming does not increase all categories of extremes uniformly. Research shows that the hottest summer days have warmed at roughly the same pace as the global median temperature, while the coldest summer days have warmed more slowly, producing a stretching of the temperature distribution and greater variability in practice. Future projections under high-emission scenarios anticipate tropical hot day amplification emerging between 2024 and 2099. Compound events — where, for instance, drought preconditions a heatwave, or antecedent soil saturation compounds a flood — represent an emergent frontier, as warming alters not just event intensity but event co-occurrence probabilities across continental scales.

Ocean Heat Content

Earth’s Energy Imbalance — the difference between incoming solar radiation absorbed by Earth and outgoing thermal radiation — is the fundamental measure of planetary heat accumulation. A persistently positive EEI means the planet is continuously storing energy, primarily in the oceans. Recent analyses confirm that the decadal trend in EEI has been increasing, amplifying the oceanic heat reservoir available to fuel weather systems.

The 2023–2024 climate anomalies, described by UN Secretary-General António Guterres as a “master class in climate destruction,” were shaped substantially by this elevated baseline. Research found that when ENSO forcing is superimposed upon an already high positive EEI, the result is amplified, record-breaking impacts well beyond what ENSO phase alone would predict. If the positive EEI trend is sustained, natural variability cycles will increasingly drive climate extremes to unprecedented magnitudes.

Storm Intensification

Elevated sea surface temperatures (SSTs) directly energize tropical cyclones by increasing available oceanic heat flux to the atmosphere. The relationship between warming SSTs and rapid intensification (RI) events — defined as a storm strengthening by 30 knots or more within 24 hours — is now well-documented across multiple ocean basins. Translation speed changes further modulate this: as tropical cyclones slow, they spend more time over warm water and induce less cooling through ocean mixing, allowing greater intensity to develop.

Ocean-Atmosphere Teleconnections

Fundamental Modulator

The ENSO is the dominant mode of interannual climate variability and a primary large-scale driver of extreme weather globally. ENSO modulates atmospheric circulation through changes in the Walker Circulation, the Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ), and the subtropical jet streams, producing basin-wide patterns of altered precipitation, temperature, and tropical cyclone activity.

Many of the extreme rainfall and drought events of 2024 were linked to atmospheric configurations associated with El Niño during winter 2023–2024. ENSO does not, however, deterministically control individual events; stochastic weather-scale variability remains a key ingredient.

Tropical Cyclone Intensification

Research provided a systematic global analysis of ENSO’s influence on tropical cyclone rapid intensification. During El Niño, RI events increase significantly in the eastern North Pacific, western North Pacific, and South Pacific, while the Atlantic experiences the opposite pattern due to enhanced vertical wind shear. During La Niña, these basin-level patterns approximately reverse.

The teleconnection mechanisms identified include changes in large-scale vertical wind shear, relative humidity, and SST patterns — all of which modulate the thermodynamic favorability for genesis and intensification. ENSO’s interaction with the Indian Ocean Dipole (IOD) further shapes cyclone activity in the Arabian Sea and Bay of Bengal, with El Niño and a positive IOD creating favorable conditions for TC intensification through reduced wind shear and elevated SSTs.

ENSO Diversity

Growing recognition of ENSO’s “flavors” — including the canonical Eastern Pacific El Niño, the Central Pacific or “Modoki” variant, and analogous La Niña subtypes — complicates both prediction and attribution. Each type produces distinct teleconnection patterns and regional impacts. Classification using Archetypal Analysis of sea-level anomalies has been shown to provide cleaner discrimination of ENSO phases when SST-based indices are ambiguous (arXiv, 2024). How anthropogenic warming will alter ENSO amplitude and teleconnection structure remains one of the most contested questions in climate science, with multi-model ensembles showing high inter-model uncertainty.

Arctic Amplification, Jet Streams, and Atmospheric Blocking

Arctic Amplification

One of the most consequential regional expressions of global warming is Arctic amplification (AA): the phenomenon whereby the Arctic is warming two to three times faster than the global average. This differential warming is driven by sea-ice loss (which reduces surface albedo and dramatically increases oceanic heat release to the atmosphere), lapse rate feedbacks, and atmospheric moisture transport.

The Polar Amplification Model Intercomparison Project (PAMIP) has clarified that sea-ice loss is the critical driver of winter AA, while atmospheric heat convergence plays a larger role in summer. Robust consequences documented across models include local Arctic warming and moistening, equatorward shifts of the North Atlantic jet stream and storm track, and fewer severe cold extremes over North America.

Jet Stream Perturbation

The strength of Earth’s jet streams is governed by the meridional (equator-to-pole) temperature gradient. As the Arctic warms disproportionately, this gradient weakens, reducing jet stream wind speeds, causing the jet to meander more in large-amplitude wave patterns, and promoting stalling behavior. A weakened, more sinuous jet stream produces two critically important consequences: (1) weather systems are steered along unusual paths, and (2) weather patterns become persistently locked in place rather than propagating normally.

During the summer of 2025, the polar jet stream shifted anomalously far southward and weakened, steering larger storm systems into mid-latitude regions of the United States, contributing to intense flooding events. Combined with additional moisture supplied by warmer oceans and air masses, this configuration produced historically significant compound precipitation events.

Quasi-Resonant Amplification and Blocking

Large-scale undulations of the jet stream — Rossby waves — arise from the conservation of potential vorticity on a rotating planet. Variations in Rossby wave amplitude, phase, and propagation speed modulate temperature and precipitation across continental regions. When these waves slow or stagnate, particular weather regimes persist for weeks, producing prolonged heatwaves, cold spells, floods, and droughts.

Research published in Scientific Reports and extended by subsequent work identified quasi-resonant amplification (QRA) as a key mechanism: waves with particular wavenumbers (zonal wavenumbers 6–8) become trapped within a mid-latitude atmospheric waveguide, their amplitude amplified by resonance, leading to persistent and extreme surface weather. Evidence suggests that QRA-favorable conditions have increased in recent decades, potentially linked to Arctic amplification narrowing the thermal gradient that defines the waveguide.

A 2024 study in the Quarterly Journal of the Royal Meteorological Society found that Arctic amplification can directly enhance the modulational instability of planetary waves, resulting in amplified planetary wave activity and increased regional weather extremes. Separately, research published in Communications Earth & Environment documented enhanced weather persistence under amplified Arctic conditions, providing observational evidence for the theorized stalling mechanism.

However, a critical counterpoint exists: work by Ronalds et al. and others has confirmed the link between low Rossby wave phase speeds and extreme temperature events, while finding that observed decadal trends in phase speed did not unambiguously accompany the reduction in the low-level temperature gradient over the 1979–2018 period. This underscores ongoing scientific debate about the magnitude and consistency of the Arctic amplification → blocking pathway.

The Polar Vortex and Stratospheric Dynamics

Polar Vortex Structure

The stratospheric polar vortex — a vast cyclonic circulation centered over the Arctic stratosphere — is a critical regulator of winter weather in the Northern Hemisphere. Its strength and stability determine whether cold Arctic air remains confined poleward or intrudes into lower latitudes. Sudden Stratospheric Warming (SSW) events can disrupt the vortex, triggering surface cold air outbreaks over North America and Europe weeks later.

NOAA’s 2025 analysis documented the contrasting behavior of the polar vortex in successive winters: the 2023–2024 vortex was frequently weakened and displaced, allowing warmer air into the Arctic stratosphere, while the 2024–2025 season saw persistent intensification, with stratospheric winds at 60°N well above average — demonstrating that the vortex can modulate weather in both its disrupted and intensified states.

Stratospheric–Tropospheric Coupling

The polar vortex communicates its behavior downward through the troposphere via changes in the tropospheric jet stream and pressure patterns. A weakened vortex tends to favor a negative Arctic Oscillation (AO) state at the surface, associated with anomalous cold air outbreaks, while an intensified vortex promotes a positive AO, confining cold air to the Arctic. These couplings operate on timescales of 2–8 weeks, providing a source of extended-range predictability.

Feedback mechanisms involving snow cover and sea-ice further modulate the vortex: reduced Arctic sea-ice in autumn has been linked to increased Eurasian snow cover anomalies, which through planetary wave forcing can weaken the vortex and predispose the following winter to cold extremes over parts of the Northern Hemisphere mid-latitudes.

Concluding Remarks

Global extreme weather formation is a multi-scale phenomenon shaped by a hierarchy of interacting forcing mechanisms. The scientific evidence is unambiguous that anthropogenic climate change, through greenhouse gas emissions, represents the dominant force systematically intensifying and redistributing extremes globally. Natural modes of variability — especially ENSO and the polar vortex cycle — act as important modulators of the timing and regional expression of events. Arctic amplification and the associated disruption of jet stream dynamics constitute an increasingly important secondary forcing pathway, particularly for mid-latitude extreme weather persistence.

The events of 2023–2026 represent a convergence of these forcings at unusual magnitudes, offering both a warning of future extremes under continued warming and a living laboratory for refining multi-causal attribution methodologies. Improved observational coverage, coupled model intercomparison projects, and rapid attribution science will continue to sharpen the quantitative decomposition of these mechanisms and reduce uncertainties in projections of future extreme weather.
